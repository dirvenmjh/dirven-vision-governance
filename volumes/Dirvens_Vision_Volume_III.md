### Chapter 21: The Problem — Why Current Governance Fails

Contemporary governance systems are failing across multiple domains:

**Lost Faith**
Citizens no longer trust institutions. Trust in government, media, and traditional authority has collapsed across democracies.

**Corruption**
Centralized systems accumulate power and hidden interests. Corruption is not a bug—it's a feature of centralized governance.

**Polarization**
Electoral systems force binary choices, amplifying extremes rather than finding consensus. This creates institutional paralysis.

**Opacity**
Most important decisions happen in private with no accountability. Citizens have no visibility into how decisions are made.

**Misalignment**
Systems that decide governance have incentives misaligned with public welfare. Decision-makers profit from decisions that harm the public.

These failures are not accidental. They are structural. They are the inevitable result of centralized power architecture.

### Chapter 25: The Core Philosophy — Dirven's Principles

Dirven's approach to governance can be summarized in a few core principles:

### Prevention Over Punishment

A society should detect harm early, align incentives toward cooperation, protect cognitive integrity, ensure universal accountability, and make ethical behavior the path of least resistance.

Prevention is not about control. It is about **protecting human potential**. A society that prevents harm is a society that can evolve.

### Structure Over Trust

Trust should not depend on belief. Trust should depend on structure.

Design systems where abuse is impossible by design, not where we hope people act ethically.

### Transparency Over Secrecy

What can be measured and audited can be improved. Transparency enables accountability. Opacity enables corruption.

### Distributed Over Centralized

Power concentrated in institutions enables abuse. Power distributed across networks with aligned incentives enables freedom.

### Technical Literacy for Governance

The governance challenges of the 21st century are fundamentally technical. They require architects who understand both technology and human systems.
